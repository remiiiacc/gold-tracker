How to read this chart ▼

This chart shows two distinct regimes in the relationship between gold prices and real interest rates (TIPS yields).

REGIME 1 (2006–2021): Real rates were the primary driver of gold prices. When the Fed pushed rates negative, gold surged. When rates rose, gold fell. The regression model fit well.

REGIME 2 (2022–Present): The Fed raised real rates to the highest levels in 15 years, yet gold continued to rise. The historical relationship broke down. Central banks and emerging market buyers — who do not make investment decisions based on US Treasury yields — became the dominant marginal buyers.

THE REGIME BREAK PREMIUM: The gap between the old model's prediction and today's gold price represents the portion of gold's value that cannot be explained by interest rates alone. This premium reflects a combination of:

  • Structural repricing due to de-dollarisation
  • Central bank reserve diversification
  • Speculative momentum

A high and expanding premium is not necessarily a sell signal — it may reflect a genuine structural shift. But it does mean gold's price is increasingly dependent on factors that are harder to model and can reverse faster than rate-driven moves.
